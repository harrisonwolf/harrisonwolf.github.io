What can the engines really do?

Three questions people actually pay to answer. What does it cost in dirt to build on a sloped foothill lot? Where does a storm's rain run when it hits a bluff, and where will it erode? And if you dammed this Sierra canyon, how much water would it hold? All of it computed in an afternoon, on two engines of mine that were never built for terrain (how this was built, below).

01Study 1 · Building padFoothill lot · Fresno County · 37.08°N 119.49°W

Imagine a lot for sale in the foothills. You want to buy it and build a house, but the ground sits on a slope. How steep, exactly? How hard would it be to get a pad level, and how much dirt would have to be cut, filled, or trucked out to do it? You can't eyeball any of that, and it's most of the grading bill. It's the number you'd want before you make the offer: what does a level pad here cost in dirt?

The pad

You cut the high side down and fill the low side up until it's level. Put the pad at the balanced grade (2,089 ft) and the dirt you remove matches the dirt you need, so almost nothing leaves the site. On a slope, the trucking is where the money goes.

The dirt

Making the pad moves about 9,160 cubic yards (cut from the high side, placed on the low side), so off-site haul is essentially nothing. Every number traces to a measured point on the ground.

The water

The lot drains southwest and collects at the low corner. Put a swale or drain there before you pour; it's cheaper than fixing it after the first storm.

02Study 2 · RainwaterWest bluff · San Joaquin River rim · north Fresno

Imagine your house sits near the rim of this bluff, or you're planning to build one here. Bluffs erode, and 95 feet below runs the San Joaquin. You don't want the foundations creeping toward that edge over the years, so you need to know what every storm does to this ground: which way the rain sheds, where it gathers and cuts, and where it leaves the property. So: where does the rain actually run?

The slope

A steep west-northwest-facing bluff dropping 95 feet toward the San Joaquin, 39% at the steepest.

The runoff

There's no holding this water in place; it runs off the face. A 1-inch storm sheds roughly 604,000 gallons off the modeled slope, all of it running downhill.

Where it goes

The runoff gathers into a draw that cuts northwest down the bluff and exits toward the river. Build near the rim and that draw is the line to respect: it's where the water and the erosion go.

03Study 3 · ReservoirSan Joaquin River gorge · Sierra Nevada · 37.03°N 119.57°W

Imagine you're a public agency mapping possible reservoir sites in the Sierra National Forest. Not one canyon: a map full of candidates, and sending a surveyor, an engineer, and a crew into each one costs real money before you learn a thing. What if you could get quick, reliable numbers on every site before anyone is sent out? This gorge is one candidate. It narrows to a single outlet on the south, and walling that outlet fills the canyon: dam the outlet, and how much would it hold?

The basin

A dry Sierra gorge that falls to a single narrow outlet on the south. Close that notch with a 256-foot dam and the canyon behind it becomes a reservoir, held on the other three sides by its own rim.

The water it holds

At full pool the basin holds 44,457 acre-feet, about 14.5 billion gallons. The stage–storage curve says what each extra ten feet of dam buys, so the dam can be sized to the need.

What it is worth

This is the first-pass number a city, county, or irrigation district pays a consultant for: how much a site holds and how tall the dam must be. My engine produces it from public data in an afternoon, before anyone surveys a thing.

Why this exists

This is a portfolio piece, a working demonstration rather than a product. I build software from scratch, close to the metal, and here I aim two of my own engines at real Sierra ground to answer three practical questions, end to end.

Neither engine behind this page was built for terrain. My geospatial platform was built for real-estate market data: county records ingested into a hashed, auditable ledger and served read-only. My geometry engine was built from first principles in a bare terminal with nothing but C++ and g++ installed (low-level predicates, triangulation, Voronoi, no libraries), to see whether I could. Mapping ground was never in the picture for either of them.

This page is what happened when I pointed them at real ground anyway. Public USGS elevation goes in; the geometry engine turns it into a triangulated surface; the earthwork and drainage are computed on that surface; a real lot boundary would come from the platform. From blank page to a working, genuinely useful tool in about an hour and a half, and nearly all of it ran on my own machine on my own code (the public elevation data and the plotting aside). That speed comes from each piece being built soundly in the first place, so two of them can bolt together into something new and hold.

How this was made

01 · elevation

Real USGS 10-meter elevation, sampled across each site: 100 points on the pad, 576 on the bluff, and 3,721 across the reservoir basin.

02 · surface

The points are triangulated into a continuous surface (a TIN) by a C++ geometry engine I built from scratch. 162, 1,058, and 7,200 triangles, exact to Euler's formula.

03 · analysis

From that surface: cut and fill balanced to a pad, and rainfall routed downhill neighbor-to-neighbor to find where water concentrates.

The computation is the substance; the 3D view is just a plot of it. The triangulation, the balanced cut-and-fill, and the reservoir capacity all ran on my own machine, in engines I wrote from scratch in C++ with no geometry libraries; the drainage analysis I wrote in Python, on that same surface. The public elevation data and the plotting library are the only pieces I didn't write.

County GIS · layer ready

It runs on the same engine as my county GIS platform, so a lot boundary can come from official parcel data, and either study runs on any parcel in the county from its address alone.

A demonstration of the method on real public data, not a licensed survey or construction document. Locations are approximate; satellite imagery courtesy Esri World Imagery. Ten-meter data suits planning and early pricing, and the same pipeline reads a surveyed point cloud without change.
